1 bash

Short answer: no — I wouldn't replace citation count with FWCI as the primary rank for this dataset. They answer different questions, and in this corpus FWCI has some disqualifying problems. But field-normalization is a real concern worth addressing a different way. Here's the reasoning, grounded in what the data actually shows:

What the data says

1. Your selection is already conditioned on raw citations. This corpus was pulled sort=cited_by_count:desc. Look at the per-domain medians:

Domain median FWCI median citations
Physical Sciences 64.9 6,680
Social Sciences 81.8 6,611
Life Sciences 71.5 6,453
Health Sciences 137.1 6,216

The domains are already balanced at the median citation level (~6,200–6,700 across all four) — because you filtered on citations. Re-ranking that same sample by FWCI is a bit incoherent: you'd be conditioning on citations to build the set, then optimizing a different objective inside it. The papers that FWCI is designed to surface — modest raw counts but exceptional for their field — were never pulled into the top-10k in the first place. So FWCI-ranking here has built-in survivorship bias.

2. FWCI amplifies the exact metadata errors that plague the raw-count ranking — it doesn't fix them. FWCI = actual citations ÷ expected citations for that field+year+type. When a paper's year is wrong (which we've now seen repeatedly — "Attention" tagged 2025, GANs, etc.), the denominator collapses and FWCI explodes. The top-by-FWCI list is worse than top-by-citations:

  • 1 "Generative Adversarial Nets", FWCI 26,990, dated 2023 (real paper: 2014 — year error inflating it)

  • 2/#4/#10 three separate "Harvard business review" container-junk records with FWCI 7k–24k

  • 6 title null, #5 a Japanese-titled calibration-curve reprint

FWCI's dynamic range is enormous (p50≈80, max≈27,000) and it's extremely sensitive to the denominator, so bad metadata gets amplified, not smoothed.

3. Coverage gap. 282 papers (2.8%) have no FWCI at all and would silently drop out of a pure-FWCI ranking. Raw cited_by_count is present for everything.

What I'd actually recommend

Keep raw citations as the primary rank/size encoding — for a citation-graph viz it's the honest primitive (the edges are real citations; node size should reflect the actual structure). Then address field bias explicitly, without FWCI's noise:

  • Best: select/rank within domain — e.g. top-N per field, or percentile-within-field-and-decade. Gives explicit, interpretable field balance with none of FWCI's denominator blowups.
  • Good: use FWCI as a secondary channel — a color scale or a filter ("show papers that over-performed their field"), not the ranking. That captures the "punched above its weight" story where it's genuinely interesting.
  • If you do want a normalized headline metric, a capped log-FWCI (clamp at ~p99, drop nulls) is far more robust than raw FWCI.

So: FWCI is a good lens, a bad primary sort here. Want me to add a --rank {citations,fwci,within-field} mode to the explore script so you can eyeball the three side by side?
